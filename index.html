Strategic Urban Visions

Industria presents a year-long research project by graduates of the Dublin School of Architecture. The project is a collaboration between the Dublin School of Architecture and IDA Ireland to investigate possibilities for foreign direct investment in the city of Waterford and to explore the future of the workplace in twenty-first century Ireland.

Urbanised Group Selection

Ard Na Ri

The group consists of three projects situated on Sion hill overlooking the city of Waterford. The urban strategy connects the projects back to the city centre. The landscape is isolated and underdeveloped, the projects enliven this area through a series of pedestrian walkways. A pedestrian lift allows public access to the hillside aimed to attract people from the city and visitors arriving at the train station. The projects consist of a drone factory, a productive agricultural landscape and a honey bee conservation centre. The urban proposal reinvigorates the landscape, painting a barren plain with wildflower and gorse.

Ballybricken

The Ballybricken group address the history and topography of their urban quarter. Ballybricken was once a bustling commercial centre of Waterford City with livestock trading taking place in the square. Today it is largely desolate with priority given to traffic over the pedestrian and few signs of a community or working spirit. The square is enlivened by providing a terraced outdoor music venue, creating a new community playground and re-locating green areas. The projects consist of a craft community, a collaborative work space and a music centre. The central space would be used by the local community to host seasonal events.

The Belview Port

The Belview port group aim to give Belview, the port of Waterford, a more visible presence in the city centre. They propose to add a series of chimneys to Belview to accompany the SmartPly factory chimney and to densify the industries of the port in closer proximity. The projects include a brettstapel factory, a magnesium oxide plant, a maritime school and a waste to energy incinerator. The urban strategy promotes a scheme for co-location, where relevant supplies are placed in proximity to the industry they support. This includes placing an industrial farm beside the Glanbia plant and providing forestry adjacent to the timber plants. In order to encourage the public to visit the port, the main axis to the port is transformed from a roadway to a tree-lined boulevard with cycle lanes and paths for pedestrians. The port itself is developed to incorporate a plaza with public amenities.

City Wall

The City Wall group are located in the medieval fabric of the city centre. The scheme intends to fold working life back into the heart of the declining city centre. The projects cover a range of varied workplaces: a pharmaceutical research facility and church; a glassblowers workshop; office buildings; a craft hat-making centre; a games development studio and gaming test centre and craft markets. The urban strategy establishes an east-west pedestrian route through the quarter and introduces a series of new public spaces. Parking is provided for at the top of the hill to relieve the centre of car-parking spaces, freeing it up to the pedestrian.

City Quays

The city quays group include a festival centre on the south quays, a hotel, a vertical brewery, a mannequin factory and a fabrication centre. They aim to redefine the city skyline with a series of towers. The hotel has eleven storeys and the vertical brewery has seventeen storeys. The new skyline is an effort to give the south quays a facelift as a new centre of industry and commerce in the 21st century. The urban strategy also aims to permeate the block of buildings facing onto the quays, as a reaction to the current disjuncture between the quays and the city centre, with a series of pedestrian routes through the new buildings on the quays.

King's Channel

The King's channel's urban strategy connects two isolated projects to the east of Waterford's centre. The strategy is based on an intrigue on the placement of industrial architecture in scenic locations. The urban strategy is themed 'curious islands' with one project, a colourworks, being situated on little island and the other, a laboratory glass factory, being isolated from the city in an 'island' of trees. The urban strategy proposes to allow access through the woodlands for curious pedestrians and extends an existing river-walk to the ferry dock to little island. The projects are strategically placed to respect and interact with the picturesque landscape they are contained in.

North Quays

The north quays of Waterford City is currently a vacant, underused frontage of what was formerly the site of ship building, grain processing and Waterford Port. The aim of the North Quays group was to populate the quay with productive industry once more and create a new elevation of the 'industrial city' across the river front. 700 permanent jobs are created with the introduction of a Ray-Ban manufacturing facility, a Fab-Lab City, a brewing community, a water purification park, an Amazon warehouse and a cruise-line terminal. A pedestrian loop was created with the addition of two pedestrian bridges at both the Ray-Ban factory and the Cruise-Line building. At various points on the pedestrian route pedestrians can interact with the river's edge. The former train line is reopened from Waterford City to the Port and beyond, as well as new pedestrian and vehicle links to the Ferrybank area.

The Social Quarter

The Social quarter group aim to revive their quarter of the city centre by re-using vacant sites and restoring valuable medieval heritage in the quarter. The projects are an entrepreneur hub in a walled garden, a poitín distillery and workspaces for tech entrepreneurs. The urban strategy dictates that each private business has a duty to give back to the city in some way, whether through the restoration of adjacent medieval fabric, the provision of new public spaces or planting vegetation in the city centre. The social quarter aims to extend the redevelopment and pedestrianisation that has already been put in place in recent years in the 'Viking Triangle' to the rest of the city.

South Bank

The South Bank, currently underdeveloped and isolated from the city centre, is the first impression many visitors get of Waterford as they arrive by car on the new motorway bridge. The urban strategy was conscious of its responsibility to create a 'welcome' to visitors or an intrigue to stop and visit the city. The projects were therefore iconic in nature and of a large scale to be striking to drivers-by. The existing ESB pylons are re-located from their prominent location to improve the area's image. The projects consist of: a machine-tool hall, a bicycle manufacturing and testing velodrome and a sports research and development centre. An abandoned train-line is put to use to service the new industries established on the south bank. With the construction of a new velodrome, bicycle lanes are introduced to connect the area to the city centre.

Urban Promenade — St. John's River

The St. John's river group aim to create an urban promenade along the floodplain of the river. The projects include a co-working space, a bespoke yacht factory, an employment and leisure centre, a youtube space, a new urban street, traditional craft workshops, the Waterford exchange and a willow farm. The projects define an urban character along the waterline, which the city currently turns its back to. They do this through the creation of walkways and public spaces along the riverfront. All the projects accommodate flooding in their design as the plain floods annually. They have also stipulated the planting of five species of tree along the riverfront: downey birch; silver birch; aspen; scot's pine and black thorn.

The Western Frontier

The western frontier consists of all the projects located on the western periphery of Waterford. The projects include a bio-fuel plant, amenities for the IDA industrial estate, an innovation centre and an exhibition hall. The urban strategy aims to react to the sprawling development which has resulted in low density and areas of high vacancy in the periphery. It aims to do this by defining edges - imposing an ecological green belt around the city centre and a further yellow belt of rapeseed fields for the bio-fuel plant around the periphery. The belts arrest urban sprawl and force the centre to re-densify. Routes into the city centre are developed with the addition of a light-rail transit which connects the WIT west campus to the city centre. At the intersection of a route and an edge an 'urban gateway' is provided: a piece of infrastructure or architecture that signifies you are entering or leaving the city.
